Brief

In July 2022, the UK Parliament approved Oasis Restore, a secure school for youth offenders aged 16–19 in Medway, Kent. Set to open in winter of 2023, it will be run by Oasis Community Learning, a charity supporting at-risk children. The school follows restorative justice principles, focusing on dialogue and addressing the needs of both offenders and victims. A significant challenge is gaining the trust of incoming students (young offenders) who arrive with negative perceptions of authority.

Intervention & Impact

To address this, we designed the Shared Experience Mentor (SEM) Program; a dedicated mentorship initiative aimed at building trust, fostering resilience, and advocating for students. The SEM will work closely with Oasis' teachers to ensure that each child's unique needs are met by tailoring lesson plans and activities to the child's needs. This approach prioritizes empathetic mentorship over behavioral enforcement, preparing the youth for a better life outside the system.

My Role

Worked with a team of three to define Oasis’ mission, co-design solutions with educators and justice stakeholders, and create service blueprints, staff guides, and mentorship tools. The project was presented to the Principal Director of Oasis Restore and representatives of the UK Ministry of Justice.

Discovery

Conducted interviews with key stakeholders to understand the UK's youth justice system. Analysed the space where the secure school is to be built to visualize how its infrastructure embodies Oasis' mission rooted in restorative justice.

To gain a comprehensive understanding of the issues within the system, our research approach involved speaking with a diverse range of stakeholders, including representatives from the justice system, educators, and other subject matter experts. While synthesizing our research, we derived valuable insights into the experiences of children within and outside of the justice system, as well as the systemic issues and challenges faced by educators and representatives of the justice system.

Problem Definition

The under-representation of neurodiversity in the justice system has resulted in a lack of understanding and support for neurodivergent young people. The behavior of young children in the justice system is often viewed as criminal and punished without understanding the underlying cause of their actions.

01

Despite having an overwhelmingly sizeable neurodivergent population in prison. the Justice System does not understand neurodiversitv and doesn't consider it in its policies.

02

Children in the justice system have been made to feel like they had no future academically because of their poor performance (or undiagnosed neurological condition), and therefore associate themselves with a life of degeneration, though they have other strengths

03

Children that end up within the justice system come from a long history of trauma and deprivation. Still, the justice system takes on a behavioural lens to enact change rather than an empathetic one.

Opportunity

The element of healthy relationships emerged as the 'North Star' of our design direction.

Area of Opportunity

The introduction of a new role (Shared Experience Mentor or SEM) in the justice ecosystem, will provide students with positive role models who will nurture healthy relationships with them, guiding them towards positive pathways for their future.

Theory of Change

We believe that if students have a stable and healthy relationship with a positive role model during their time in the secure school, then the guidance towards a positive life path will significantly decrease their risk of re-offending.

Design Intervention

To optimise the support provided to students within the secure school system, we proposed implementing a new role, the “Shared-Experience Mentor” (SEM). The SEM would possess a common experience with the students that they could significantly identify with to amplify the pace and depth of their connection.

Each SEM would:

  • Work closely with teachers to tailor lesson plans and activities based on individual neurodivergent profiles and trauma histories

  • Support students in processing past trauma, building trust, and forming healthy relationships

  • Empower children to feel secure, supported, and prepared for life beyond the justice system

Prototyping & Workshops

Facilitated co-creation workshops and developed artefacts for the SEM to engage with incoming students within the Oasis compound, enabling the mentors to understand, empower and build healthy relationships with the young students.

Key Learning: Though the tools are effective in facilitating engagement, the mentor's natural rapport and ability to connect with children was a more significant factor in the success of the relationship. As a result, our research shifted towards refining the hiring process for mentors rather than solely improving the usability of the tools.

Outcomes

Developed a mentorship program and suit of tools to aid the SEM (Shared Experience Mentor) in their role. The SEM would serve as a friend, confidant, and advocate for the students, while also acting as a mediator between the students and staff during escalated situations.

Impact

We are not just introducing a new role, we are changing the organisational dynamics of Oasis. Because the SEM will understand the child, the other staff can leverage this understanding and provide better support. By working together, the whole will be greater than the sum of its parts. In the future, we envision a whole network of people who leave the secure school and come together to form one body.
